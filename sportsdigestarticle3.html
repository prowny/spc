How to be a Good Sport Parent

 

By Sheila K. Rowny

 

Director, Sports Psychotherapy Center

 

If you watched the Boys 16 & 18 Super National Clay Court Championships recently played at our local clubs, you probably saw some great tennis.  You may also have witnessed some boys throwing rackets and bags, or swearing loudly.  At another tennis match some years ago, one parent violently assaulted another parent while watching their sons play.  On the sidelines of any soccer or Little League game we all observe a range of player and parent attitudes.  No one likes to lose, and most people don’t like to lose their temper.  Yet both things happen.  While we can’t control the outcome of a sports event, we can control our own behavior.  Truly successful competitive athletes must learn good sportsmanship, and their parents need to set an example of behavior which is polite, respectful, and honorable. 

How do we instill the best attitudes in our young athletes?  Here are some guidelines:

  • Understand that sports is just a game.  It’s not a job or a business.  Kids play sports primarily to have fun, to enjoy the challenge, and for social camaraderie.  Focus on your child’s enjoyment, not your own reasons for your child to play sports.  If you are more invested in your child’s participation than he or she is, there will be problems.
  • Before lessons, practice, or competitions:  Ask your child whether he or she prefers you to watch, or to keep your distance.  If you are allowed on the sidelines, be unobtrusive, refrain from coaching, and stay calm and neutral.  Parents who hover, instruct, cheer or jeer put extra pressure on a young athlete.  This can lead to tension and mistakes, or even cheating.
  • Children need to know you still love them even when they play badly or lose a competition.  If you avoid emphasizing results, kids will get that you care for them more than their trophies.  Ask questions like “Did you have fun?  Did you try your hardest?” instead of “Did you win?”
  • Comfort your child after a loss but remember that losing is part of a necessary learning process.  Most athletes, however talented, will eventually be beaten by someone better.  Kids need time to be upset, then get over it.  Parents can help by remaining calm and supportive, no matter the outcome.  After a useful cooling off period, encourage your child to think about what went on during the match.  By talking about what went right and wrong, players appreciate their strengths and weaknesses, and parents also learn what their child is thinking.  Maybe this will be different than you expect, and different from your own reactions.

            What if you’ve sacrificed so much time and money on your child’s sport and he or she wants to quit?  Your child may be tired, injured, frustrated or bored.  He or she may need to take a break from competing; try some different sports; develop other social activities that are stress-free.   If your child ultimately decides to abandon the sport, consider that you still have made a substantial investment in his or her development as a person.

            Here are some resources for sports parents:

  • National Alliance for Youth Sports  (www.NAYS.org)
  • Parents Association for Youth Sports provides a handbook and code of ethics (www.paysonline.nays.org)
  • Center for Sports Parenting  publishes “Top 10 Rules of Parental Behavior” at kids’ games  (www.sportsparenting.org)

 

 

Sheila Rowny directs the Sports Psychotherapy Center in Bethesda, Maryland, where she teaches mental strategies to teenage and adult athletes who want to achieve Peak Performance.
