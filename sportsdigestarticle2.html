Conquer The Fear Factor

By Sheila K. Rowny

Director, Sports Psychotherapy Center

“I keep making mistakes… what if I lose the next game… uh oh, my bad knee is acting up… who is playing on the next hole… I hope I don’t choke like I did before…”

There are many reasons why an excellent athlete plays poorly or below his or her usual level of ability.  One very powerful reason is fear, also known as anxiety or “nerves”.

Most competitive athletes experience some kind of fear, or anxiety, or worry.  This is normal!  It arises at different times for different players.  Some get an upset stomach the night before a big match, or vomit when it’s over, win or lose.  Some get nervous before a big point, with negative thoughts:  “I’m going to lose.”  Some just get all tensed up and make unforced errors when they can’t control their bodies.  Or they make poor choices when under pressure.

Some people are “natural-born worriers.”  Even when not playing their sport they tend to think ahead or dwell on past problems, expect the worst, worry about taking a test, a risk, or performing at school or work.  Thinking of past or future problems destroys the self-confidence and focus on the present which is necessary for playing like a champion.  People who are perfectionists also tend to develop a fear of losing.  They don’t understand that sports, like life, is full of ups and downs. 

Suppose you started entering tournaments at age 10 and won easily and often.  You advanced to higher levels, winning effortlessly, believing yourself to be a champ, until you got beaten for the first time by someone bigger, older, or just better that day.  The reaction to that loss by yourself, parents, coach or friends can have a big impact on future competition.  If you experience a negative response to this loss, you are more likely to approach the next match with fear – not because of your loss but because of your negative interpretation of the meaning of that loss.  A negative interpretation could be from inside yourself – “I’m such a loser” – or seeing a parent’s frown or scowl – “you should have won” – or hearing a coach’s critique.  You conclude that you can’t play and everyone is disappointed in you.  And instead of getting over it, you carry fear and negative feelings about yourself to the next match.  Most of the time these reactions involve distorted conclusions about your performance.

Here are some Action Plans that will move you beyond fear:

  1.  Goals - set a very specific and immediate goal, and focus only on achieving this goal, right now.  For example: “I will serve and volley this point.”  “I will aim for the green.”

  2. Rituals – follow the exact same physical and mental routine before and after every point.  For example, bounce the ball 3 times, or take a deep breath, or look at the sky, or say to yourself “just concentrate on this shot.”
  3. Positive Affirmation – identify your negative thoughts: “that was stupid”, or your tense body; and replace it with a positive affirmation.  These are examples of positive thoughts to practice both during and between matches:

I am confident                                       I am determined

I love this game                                     I trust myself to play my best

I am alert and focused                           I’ll stick to my plan

            Notice how your emotions change and how your body feels when you try these positive statements.  Pick one or two that you will use during matches to help you conquer your fears.  Notice that these affirmations are not about the score or the outcome.  You cannot control those things, only your own mind and body.

Remember that when you notice a negative thought or behavior, it is important to recognize it, without judging or criticizing yourself for having that thought.  Just calmly and gently replace it with a more useful positive affirmation.

  1. Maintain Balance In Your Life.  Engage in other fun activities and spend time with positive people outside of your sport.  Your identity and self-worth are based on more than wins and losses.  Off the tennis court or the golf course, you can improve your attitude by gaining some distance and new perspective.  By doing so, you will conquer the Fear Factor and perform your personal best during competition.

Sheila Rowny directs the Sports Psychotherapy Center in Bethesda, Maryland, where she teaches mental strategies to teenage and adult athletes who want to achieve Peak Performance.
