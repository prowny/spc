The Importance of the Mental Game in Sports

By Sheila K. Rowny

Director, Sports Psychotherapy Center

In the 1993 women’s finals at Wimbledon, Jana Novotna was ahead 4-1 in the third set and serving at 40-30.  She was five points from winning the title from Steffi Graf.  She double-faulted, then made a series of errors.  Now Graf was ahead 5-4 and Novotna’s head was down, her toss too low, her movements slowed.  Receiving serve she rocked and jumped, eyes darting around the court.   More unforced errors, the eventual loss, then tears.  The elite player had crumbled under intense pressure.

            The Olympic high-diver Greg Lougainis slipped on the diving board, hit his head and plunged into the pool.  Judges score: 0.  Mopping the blood dripping from the gash, Greg went into the locker room, placed earphones on his head for a few minutes, then returned to perform a perfect second dive for the gold medal.  (Only much later did the world also learn that he was HIV-positive.)

            These are two vivid examples of the power of the mind to influence and determine athletic performance.  Novotna’s story illustrates the power to defeat oneself.  Lougainis demonstrated the mental strength to transcend what appeared to be overwhelming obstacles.  He achieved a flawless performance, and the highest reward.

            What is the difference between these two superb athletes?  Sure - - male vs. female, diving vs. tennis.  I could have used examples that matched up better.   But the real difference between their experiences was their mental attitude in response to enormous competitive stress.  Jana choked.  Greg rose to the challenge.

            Most world-class athletes acknowledge and accept the importance of the mental aspect of their sport.  They incorporate mental training into their overall preparation for competition.  Even professional sports teams have utilized the knowledge and skills learned from sports psychology to enhance their performance as individuals and team members.  Whether soccer or basketball, golf or tennis, the best players know their mental game is inseparable from the physical game; when the mental and physical are in balance, athletes play their best. 

            So what is the Mental Game?  Sports Psychology?  The Zone?  Mental Toughness?  You’ve heard the terms but may not know what they really mean.  You may even agree that the concept is important enough to learn and master, but don’t know exactly how to do that.

            Many researchers, coaches, and athletes have defined and described variations on the mental game in sports.  Timothy Gallwey was one of the first (and best) to write about “The Inner Game of Tennis” in 1974.  In the introduction to that book he says “the inner game is the game that takes place in the mind of the player, and it is played against such obstacles as lapses in concentration, nervousness, self-doubt and self-condemnation.  In short, it is played to overcome all habits of mind which inhibit excellence in performance.”

            The late Charles M. Schulz, creator of “Peanuts” cartoons, was also a wise sports psychologist.  In one cartoon strip, Snoopy is watching Woodstock attempt to fly from the roof of his doghouse: “Now when you take off, do you push with your feet?  Or do you flap your wings first?  Do you sort of lean into it, or do you…” KLUNK! (Woodstock has fallen to the ground.)  Snoopy says: “If you think about it, you can’t do it.”

            The mental game involves all the thoughts and feelings going on inside your head - - before, during, and after a competition - - whether you are consciously aware of them or not.  It includes thoughts and feelings which may not even seem to be related to your game.  For example, if you’re feeling hungry between points, or thinking about a TV show during a time-out - - these are indications that you are distracted from your playing and have lost your concentration.  (In future articles I will address the subject of how to identify and overcome negative or distracting thoughts and feelings.)  Any negative or critical attitudes - - about yourself, opponent, teammate, the weather - - diminish your ability to play your best.  Athletes do not perform well when involved in self-judgment, self-criticism, or analytical (“What happened there?” “Why?”) thoughts.  You perform best when you are “out of your mind”: playing automatically and instinctively.  Athletes who have experienced “playing in the zone” often use these descriptions: “mindless…effortless…time stood still…intensely focused and relaxed at the same time.”  While these phrases may sound mystical or mysterious, there are specific skills which anyone can learn and practice in order to achieve such a positive mental state and optimal athletic performance.  To play your best sports you must develop and maintain these mental traits: concentration, self-confidence, and a love of the challenge.  You focus on playing your personal best rather than on the outcome of winning.   You can work extremely hard and still have fun.  And you have prepared sufficiently so you can trust your body to do what it knows how to do - - without your having to tell it what to do during competition.

            In future articles you will learn a range of positive thought patterns and behaviors you can practice in order to improve your focus and concentration, and build your self-confidence.  Future articles will cover:

How to focus your eyes and your thoughts

            How to “get over it” – dealing with distractions

            How to control your breathing and yourself, and forget the rest

            Using mental rehearsal to achieve optimal performance

The ability to control your thoughts and feelings under the pressure of competition, combined with good coaching, consistent physical training, and dedicated drilling, will lead to greater mental toughness and flexibility.  And you won’t defeat yourself as Jana Novotna did.
