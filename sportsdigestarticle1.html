If You Think About It, You Can’t Do It.

By Sheila K. Rowny

Director, Sports Psychotherapy Center

 

            What is the Mental Game?  Sports Psychology?  The Zone?  Mental Toughness?  You’ve heard the terms but may not know what they really mean.  You may even agree that the concept is important enough to learn, but don’t know exactly how to do that.

            Many researchers, coaches, and athletes have talked about the mental game in sports.  Timothy Gallwey was one of the first to write about “The Inner Game of Tennis” in 1974.  In the introduction to that book he says “the inner game is the game that takes place in the mind of the player, and it is played against such obstacles as lapses in concentration, nervousness, self-doubt and self-condemnation.  In short, it is played to overcome all habits of mind which inhibit excellence in performance.”

            The late Charles M. Schulz, creator of “Peanuts” cartoons, was also a wise sports psychologist.  In one cartoon strip, Snoopy is watching Woodstock attempt to fly from the roof of his doghouse: “Now when you take off, do you push with your feet?  Or do you flap your wings first?  Do you sort of lean into it, or do you…” KLUNK! Woodstock has fallen to the ground.  Snoopy says: “If you think about it, you can’t do it.”

            The mental game involves all the thoughts and feelings going on inside your head - - before, during, and after a competition - - whether you are consciously aware of them or not.  Any negative attitudes - - about yourself, opponent, teammate, the weather - - diminish your ability to play your best.  Athletes do not perform well when involved in self-judgment, self-criticism, or analytical (“What happened there?” “Why?”) thoughts.  You perform best when you are playing automatically and instinctively.  Athletes who have experienced “playing in the zone” often use these descriptions: “mindless…effortless…time stood still…intensely focused and relaxed at the same time.”  While these phrases may sound mystical or mysterious, there are specific skills which anyone can learn and practice in order to achieve such a positive mental state and optimal athletic performance.  To play your best, you must develop and maintain these mental traits: concentration, self-confidence, and a love of the challenge.  You focus on playing your personal best, rather than on the outcome of winning.  Just as you feel awkward when trying a new stroke or grip, mental skills require time and practice before they feel natural.  When you have prepared sufficiently, you can trust your body to do what it knows how to do - - without your having to tell it what to do during competition. 

The ability to control your thoughts and feelings under the pressure of competition, combined with good coaching, consistent physical training, and dedicated drilling, will lead to great sports performance.  When the mental and physical games are in balance, athletes play their best.

Sheila Rowny directs the Sports Psychotherapy Center in Bethesda, Maryland, where she teaches mental strategies to teenage and adult athletes who want to achieve Peak Performance.
